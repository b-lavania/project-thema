### Goals of a PMOS

**Product Manager Operating System (PMOS) Specification**

**Version**: 1.0 (Compiled from original 2022 thread by Rick Jarrell @heyrickjarrell)  
**Purpose**: A practical, reusable toolkit of templates, frameworks, and approaches for Product Managers. Designed for portability across roles and companies, with heavy emphasis on collaboration tools like Notion and Figma. Draws inspiration from Basecamp, Amazon, lean startup methodologies, and other proven sources.  
**Goals of PMOS**:
- Toolkit applicable to any role (with situational tweaks).
- Rapid understanding of the product environment.
- Built-in, ready-to-use, highly collaborative applications.

### 1. Business Model Canvas
**Description**: A foundational lean startup tool for understanding how and why a business operates. Maps key elements of the business model in a single visual.  
**Use Cases**: 
- Onboarding to a new product or company.
- Validating assumptions about value creation, delivery, and capture.
- Strategic alignment sessions.  
**Implementation Tips**: Populate collaboratively in tools like Notion, Miro, or Figma. Update iteratively as discovery progresses.  
**Visual Reference**: Standard Business Model Canvas layout (9 blocks: Key Partners, Key Activities, Key Resources, Value Propositions, Customer Relationships, Channels, Customer Segments, Cost Structure, Revenue Streams).

### 2. Roadmap
**Description**: Balances high-confidence short-term deliverables with inspirational long-term vision.  
**Preferred Approach**: 6-week planning cycles (inspired by Basecamp, Intercom). Combines Now/Next/Later structure with outcome-oriented goals.  
**Key Principles**:
- Short-term items: High confidence and executable.
- Longer-term: Inspirational to maintain excitement.
- Avoid overly rigid timelines for complex products.  
**Alternatives**: Notion’s Roadmap template (with sprints and sub-tasks) for stakeholders who prefer due dates.  
**Implementation Tips**: Use in Notion or similar for collaboration. Visual timelines over spreadsheets/Jira for stakeholder communication.

### 3. PRD (Product Requirements Document)
**Description**: Primary tool for alignment across teams. Keeps focus on shared understanding rather than exhaustive documentation.  
**Core Attributes**:
- Short, simple, and digestible by technical and non-technical audiences.
- Emphasizes execution details.  
**Recommended Template**: Kevin Yien’s PRD (Google Doc example referenced in thread). Evolve it per project needs; not one-size-fits-all.  
**Use Cases**: Feature definition, cross-functional kickoffs, and maintaining alignment throughout development.  
**Philosophy**: “Don’t overthink it.” Prioritize clarity and brevity.

### 4. Mock Press Releases / FAQs (Amazon Working Backwards)
**Description**: Part of Amazon’s writing culture. Starts with the customer outcome by drafting a press release and FAQ before building.  
**Benefits**:
- Forces clear thinking.
- Encodes innovation discipline.
- Useful for new product pitches.  
**Philosophy**: More than a template — a mindset of working backwards from the desired customer experience.  
**Reference**: Trung Phan’s overview of Amazon’s approach (6-page memos instead of PowerPoint).

### 5. Story Mapping
**Description**: User-centric visualization of the product experience.  
**Advantages**:
- Easier to comprehend than spreadsheets or Jira boards.
- Represents the user journey rather than internal business priorities.  
**Recommended Approach**: Jeff Patton’s story mapping method (endorsed by John Cutlefish).  
**Use Cases**: Discovery workshops, backlog organization, and communicating user flows.  
**Implementation**: Whiteboard-style in Figma, Miro, or Notion for collaborative mapping.

### 6. KPI Tree
**Description**: Hierarchical view linking product work to broader business impact.  
**Use Cases**: Especially valuable in growth roles. Clarifies how features drive metrics.  
**Reference**: Melinda Elmborg’s example for structure.  
**Implementation Tips**: Build as a simple tree diagram or spreadsheet. Update regularly to track contribution.

### 7. Planning Timeline
**Description**: Acknowledges real-world deadlines while minimizing overhead.  
**Principles**:
- Product and engineering teams often resist heavy timelines.
- Keep it simple; focus on key milestones.
- Strictly avoid complex Gantt charts.  
**Use Cases**: Stakeholder communication, dependency management, and external commitments.  
**Tips**: Lightweight formats (e.g., simple tables or Now/Next/Later extensions).

### 8. Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD) Framework
**Description**: Focuses on understanding the underlying reasons customers “hire” your product.  
**Core Application**: Convert visualizations into a living spreadsheet updated during discovery.  
**Benefits**: Shifts focus from features to customer jobs, pains, and gains.  
**Visual**: Standard JTBD diagram (Jobs, Pains, Gains, etc.).  
**Use Cases**: Customer interviews, feature ideation, and prioritization.

### 9. ICE Prioritization
**Description**: Quick heuristic for ranking features using Impact, Confidence, and Ease (Sean Ellis).  
**Usage Guidelines**:
- Best for small sets of comparable features.
- Heuristic, not rigid formula.
- Helps move from discussion to execution quickly.  
**Reference**: Detailed breakdown in Nimay’s Medium article (linked via Shreyas Doshi).  
**Implementation**: Simple scoring table in a spreadsheet or Notion database.

### Additional Framework: DACI Decision Making
**Description**: Structured roles for group decisions (Driver, Approver, Contributors, Informed).  
**Benefits**: Accelerates decision-making in underperforming or slow teams.  
**Reference**: Atlassian’s framework.  
**Use Cases**: Prioritization debates, strategy choices, pricing, SEO, etc.  
**Implementation**: Document in meeting notes or dedicated decision logs.

**Recommendations for Use**:
- Customize per company context (e.g., enterprise vs. startup).
- Maintain in collaborative tools for team access.
- Regularly review and evolve the PMOS as new frameworks emerge.
- Combine tools fluidly (e.g., JTBD into PRD, Story Mapping into Roadmap).

This structured PMOS serves as a comprehensive, portable operating system for product management, emphasizing clarity, user focus, speed, and alignment.


### The frameworks

1. **Business Model Canvas** — lean startup staple; understand how and why the business works
2. **Roadmap** — high confidence near-term + inspiration long-term; 6-week cycles (Basecamp, Intercom); now/next/later with outcomes
3. **PRD** — alignment over comprehensiveness; short and digestible (Kevin Yien execution style)
4. **Mock press releases** — Amazon working backwards; customer-first forcing function
5. **Story mapping** — user journey view of the roadmap (Jeff Patton via John Cutler)
6. **KPI tree** — connect product work to business impact (growth roles)
7. **Planning timeline** — real-world deadlines without Gantt chart obsession
8. **Jobs-To-Be-Done** — why customers actually want the product; living spreadsheet through discovery
9. **ICE prioritization** — Impact × Confidence × Ease to stop endless debate
10. **DACI** — Driver, Approver, Contributors, Informed for faster decisions

Jarrell's thread title says nine; planning timeline and DACI are both called out as separate tools in the unroll.
