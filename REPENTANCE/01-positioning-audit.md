# Positioning Audit — Where the Real Value Lives

A positioning audit of `master_context.md`. The context file is rich on *what you did* (projects, problems, metrics) and thin on *why anyone should care*. This document extracts the value layer underneath the project list — the thing you actually sell to founders, hiring managers, and future teammates.

---

## The core problem with the current narrative

The master context reads as a **catalog of projects**. Every role is a well-told story, but the reader has to assemble your value themselves. Four personas (AI PM, Platform PM, Growth PM, HR Tech PM) pull in four directions. A skeptical hiring manager skims it and concludes "capable generalist, lots of small companies" — which undersells the one thing that is genuinely rare about you.

You are not selling projects. You are selling a **repeatable cognitive move**.

---

## Where the real skill is (the through-line)

Strip the domains away and the same move repeats in every single role:

| Role | Stated problem | What you reframed it to |
|------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Netsweeper | "Detection accuracy too low" | **Operational trust** — froze features, pruned false positives |
| Village Wellth | "Pricing/monetization" | **Decision architecture** — killed tiers, not labels |
| BVXpress | "Need deeper analysis" | **Presentation/export speed** — pivoted roadmap to the client-facing step |
| Moovez | "Compete on price" | **Speed-to-quote** — out-quote, don't out-price |
| BVXpress HRIS | "Replace the HR stack" | **Integrate, don't replace** — adapter pattern over migration |

**This is the asset.** The skill is not pricing, not CV, not MMM, not HRIS. It is **diagnostic reframing**: finding the real problem behind the stated problem, then having the discipline to *cut* the obvious-but-wrong solution. Every one of your best outcomes came from refusing the brief and re-scoping it.

Three skills sit underneath and make the reframing credible:

1. **Bilingual: technical ↔ commercial.** You translate technical paradigms (OR, Computer Vision, Bayesian/MMM, PyPSA, LLM orchestration) into money levers (take rate, ARPU, CAC, fill rate). Most PMs speak one side. You operate both and can prototype the first version yourself.
2. **Product analytics as a decision engine.** You measure behavioral patterns to understand *why* customers do what they do, then use that to change what gets built. Session replay reframes funnels; cohort and retention analysis surfaces the real churn driver; attribution modeling (MMM, incrementality) connects spend to revenue. This isn't decoration — it's how you find the real problem before committing engineering.
3. **Research as a decision gate, not decoration.** Opportunity maps gate roadmap bets; tree tests kill launches before they ship; focus groups reframe the problem. Research outputs are framed as *decisions changed*, not artifacts produced.
4. **0-to-1 with the commercial engine attached.** You don't just ship the product — you build the pricing, funnel, retention, and unit economics around it. First product hire three times over.

## The brand to communicate

### The Founding Product Lead

> **I find the real problem and reduce operational ambiguity, then build the system that makes it permanent.**

Everything you've done is the same act: take a chaotic, judgment-dependent, manual process and convert it into a **structured, trustworthy, automated system**. Quoting by phone → instant CV quote. Plan confusion → 3 clean tiers. Alert noise → trusted signal. Spreadsheet diligence → normalized workforce truth.

That is the one sentence. Lead with it. Let the projects be *evidence*, not the headline.

**Spine (from concept1):** Founding / early-stage technical PM. The first product hire who ships 0-to-1 and wraps the commercial engine around it. Audience: founders, AI teams, messy-stage companies.

**Voice (from concept2):** "Are we even solving the right problem?" / reduce operational ambiguity / decision latency. Diagnostic, systems-level, outcome-first.

### Three proof pillars (always lead with these)

1. **Reframe** — turned the stated problem into the real one (4+ documented cases).
2. **Build** — CV, OR, LLM eval, MMM; ships production systems solo.
3. **Commercialize** — 3x take rate, 20x quoting, $450→$600 ARPU.

### Where your value lands — and where it doesn't

| Who is hiring | Fit | Why |
|---------------|-----|-----|
| **Early-stage founder** (Seed → Series B) | **STRONG** | They screen for breadth, speed, autonomy, "can you build without a team." Your range and 0-to-1 record are exactly what they want. Your weaknesses don't matter — there's nothing to scale yet. |
| **AI / Technical PM** roles | **STRONG** | They screen for hands-on AI judgment: model eval, prompt orchestration, knowing when AI reduces vs. adds friction. Your CV testbench and "AI reduces operational ambiguity" thesis are real signal, not buzzwords. |

| **Director / Head of Product** | **WEAK** | They screen for org-building and managing managers. You don't have it yet. Don't aim here — it exposes your biggest gap. |

**The pattern:** you align where the expectation is "own ambiguity, find the real problem, and build." You misalign where it's "you've scaled a large team." Pick the rooms where the first sentence is the job.

### One-line elevator pitch

> "I'm a founding product lead. I join messy, early-stage products, find the real problem behind the stated one — using analytics and research, not instinct — and build the system that makes the fix stick. I've done it as employee #3, #6, and as the only product hire."

## Narrative Risk Mitigation & Exit Strategy

To sell the **Founding Product Lead** position, your exit narrative and operational reality must be airtight:

- **What you actually are:** An operator who took a real logistics business, identified the automation opportunity, and drove AI systems into production (CV pipeline, OR engine, conversational agent, multi-model evaluation framework). You didn't found it, but you built the product. That's a legitimate operator credential, not a consulting project.
- **The exit narrative:** "I built Quotely's AI systems from scratch and proved they work in production, and I'm now blocked by an absentee CTO who can't execute." This is a clean exit narrative. It's a resource constraint problem, not a capability problem.
- **What it unlocks:** Series-A to Series-B AI-native companies in logistics, supply chain, or B2B operations automation—where they need someone who has shipped AI in a real operational context, not a generalist PM. Focus on domain specificity as the differentiator.
- **What it doesn't unlock:** Large company PM roles with structured hiring funnels. You still don't have the logo screening. Do not waste outbound capacity there.

### Three interview risks you must manage:

1. **Relationship validation:** Hiring managers will ask: "What was your actual title and comp structure at Moovez/Quotely?" Your relationship (employee, contractor, equity) determines whether your outcomes are credible or dismissible. Have a clean, verified answer.
2. **CTO explanation:** Why didn't you fix the co-founder bottleneck? Defensible answer: "I drove the product side but couldn't replace the technical co-founder. Things stalled on engineering execution."
3. **Small-n scale:** $500k GMV is micro-scale. Lead with system metrics (e.g., job costing from 60 to 3 min, 50% deflection, 93% fill rate), not the GMV. The operational improvement story is stronger than the GMV number.

## Weaknesses (what a skeptical reader sees)

- **Scale ceiling.** Almost everything is sub-$10M, early-stage, tiny teams (employee #3, #6; ~4 and ~40-person companies; GMV $500k+). No evidence of operating at scale — no large user base, no big-org complexity. A late-stage/FAANG hiring manager reads "small-company PM."
- **Thin people leadership.** "1 direct report," "oversaw 4 developers." No org-building, no managing managers, no team scaling. Cuts you out of Director/Head-of-Product conversations.
- **Scattered tenure / fractional sprawl.** Concurrent fractional engagements plus short stints (Liohan, Jan–May) read as unfocused. Hard to attribute durable, compounding outcomes to you specifically.
- **Metrics are soft on verification.** Many headline numbers are *projected*, *single-deployment*, or early-stage ("projected 18% CAC reduction," "85% predictive accuracy," "3 teams onboarded"). A discerning reader discounts self-reported, small-n metrics. The volume of metrics can read as compensating.
- **Four-persona dilution.** Trying to be AI PM + Platform PM + Growth PM + HR Tech PM weakens every pitch. Founders and HMs hire for a *specific shape*. Breadth without a declared lane reads as "master of none."
- **"Chief of Staff turned Product Leader" ambiguity.** The COS framing can read as "generalist operator, not a real PM." It invites the question rather than settling it.
- **Defensiveness as a tell.** The repeated insistence that the HRIS "shipped as a product, not internal-only tooling" signals an underlying credibility worry. Over-justification reads as weakness; state it once, plainly, and move on.
- **Recency concentration.** Your deepest tenure (BVXpress, 9 yrs) ended in 2021. The flagship AI work (Moovez) is one ongoing, small-scale company.

## The strategic tension (and the call to make)

The *same facts* flip value depending on the reader:

| Fact | To a founder | To a scale-stage HM |
|------|--------------|----------------------|
| Range across domains/methods | Asset — "one hire does it all" | Liability — "not deep in our domain" |
| Small companies, first product hire | Asset — "knows 0-to-1" | Liability — "never operated at scale" |
| Can build the MVP himself | Asset — "force multiplier" | Neutral / "why isn't he an eng?" |
| Fractional portfolio | Asset — "battle-tested across contexts" | Liability — "can't commit / no depth" |

**Recommendation: stop hedging across four personas. Your strongest, most honest, least-contested position is the *founding / early-stage technical PM* — the first product hire who finds the real problem and builds the commercial engine.** Lead there. Let HR Tech, Growth, and Platform be *supporting evidence of range*, not co-equal claims. Chasing the scale-stage Growth PM role is where your weaknesses are most exposed and your differentiator matters least.

The master context is "too deep" on projects. The fix is not more detail — it's a clearer top layer that tells the reader what to conclude before they read a single bullet.

The master context reads as four blurred personas. The portfolio is worse in a specific way — it presents **three distinct, non-overlapping brand identities**, each with a different headline, audience, and CTA:

| Artifact | Identity | Audience | CTA |
