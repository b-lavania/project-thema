# Too Greedy — The 12-Month Commitment Plan

The cure for `fatalism.md` is not another insight. It is a year of *not* re-rolling the dice. This plan turns the four improvement areas **compounding, distribution, outcome over insight, closing the possibility space** into one domain, one identity, one number, held for twelve months.

The title is the point: pick *one* thing and be greedy about it instead of spreading thin.

---

## The three commitments (lock these on day one)

> ### One domain: **Operational AI for logistics / field service**
> Anchored on Quotely. It is already live, already commercialized, already your most defensible technical-plus-commercial story. No new domains for 12 months — not healthcare, not HR tech, not energy. Those become *evidence of range*, never new bets.
>
> **Critical Q1 prerequisite — the dev team / CTO problem.** Holding this bet for 12 months while blocked on shipping is not compounding; it's just waiting. The absentee CTO and unreliable dev team are a structural blocker, not a productivity problem, and if it isn't resolved in Q1 the rest of this plan is built on air. The resolution has three options, in order of preference: (a) replace the CTO with a hands-on lead engineer; (b) restructure so you can ship the things that matter solo or with trusted contractors, independent of the current team; (c) shift the primary vehicle from the product to a fractional advisory / white-label playbook that doesn't require a dev team to generate revenue. The bet stays. The current team dependency has to break.
>
> **The hiring alternative (Path B):** If you transition to a full-time role to break the execution bottleneck, you must join a company with a real engineering team (5+ engineers) in place to execute against product decisions. Target Series-A startups (logistics, supply chain, dispatch/field operations) solving complex workflows, where your shipping track record is a high-value asset rather than a Seed-stage co-founder gamble.

> ### One identity: **Founding Product Lead**
> concept1's spine, concept2's voice. One portfolio, one title, one LinkedIn headline, one sentence. concept2 and CONCEPT3 get archived, not maintained.

> ### One number: **Quotely ARR** (recommended year-1 target: **$120K ARR / 10 paying operators**)
> One lagging number you own publicly. Not "3x take rate," not "85% accuracy" — those are insight metrics. ARR forces distribution, compounding, and outcome. If you take a full-time role instead, the number becomes **one revenue/retention number you own end-to-end for the full year** (not a portfolio of clever wins). Lock the exact figure on day one and do not move the goalpost.

**The anti-reframe rule:** for 12 months you may not change the domain, the identity, or the number. Reframing them *is* the relapse. Write the three on one line and pin it where you work.

## The two weaknesses you just named — and the trap in this plan

You admitted: weak at **stakeholder management** and at **backlog discipline / resisting the feature factory**. These are not new problems — they're the *internal-org* versions of gaps already in `fatalism.md`:

- **Stakeholder management = distribution aimed inward.** Same muscle as selling, pointed at the CEO, eng, customers, and your one report instead of at prospects.
- **Backlog discipline = outcome-over-insight, operationalized.** A feature factory is just work that was never tied to a number. The cure is the "what number, how much?" filter applied to *every* backlog item.

**The trap:** the founding-solo lane below conveniently has no stakeholders to manage and no contested backlog. Left as-is, this plan would let you *avoid* the two skills you just named — the operating system relapsing one level up. So the reps for both are now wired into the quarters on purpose. If you cannot run a backlog and align humans, you stay a solo operator forever and never become hireable as a product *leader* — that optionality is worth keeping open even inside the founding lane.

## Quarter-by-quarter

### Q1 (Months 1–3) — Close the possibility space
*Improvement area: commitment.*

- **Kill the fragmentation.** One portfolio site (concept1 spine). Archive concept2 and CONCEPT3. Rewrite `master_context.md`'s top block to the single thesis. One title everywhere.
- **Lock the number.** Pick the exact ARR/logo target and the date. Build the dashboard that shows it. Look at it every Monday.
- **Pick the 10 logos.** Name 10 real target operators for Quotely (or, if employed, the one number and the one team you own).
- **Leading metric:** identity is singular across all surfaces; dashboard exists; 10 named targets.
- **Explicitly NOT doing:** no new side project, no new domain exploration, no "what if I also..."

### Q2 (Months 4–6) — Reactivate the distribution muscle you walked away from
*Improvement area: distribution.*

**Corrected framing:** you owned the distribution layer at BVXpress for nine years (2012–2021) — concentrated in finance/M&A — and it worked. You left *deliberately*, via MBA and fractional consulting, to escape being permanently pigeonholed in one industry at one company. That was the right call. But it created a second-order trap: distribution now feels like the road back to the cage. So you've spent the years since building architectures that nobody had to sell, because selling-hard-in-one-domain felt identical to the thing you escaped. The fear is rational. The consequence is that you avoid distribution precisely when it would compound.

The actual rep this quarter is not *learning* to distribute — you already know how. It is distributing *in a domain you chose*, on *your own terms*, with an exit valve you control. That's the difference between BVXpress and now.

- **Sell, don't build.** You default to building because it's where the insight high lives. This quarter the constraint is reversed: ship *no* major new feature unless a paying or near-paying customer is blocked on it. Spend the saved time on outbound, demos, and closing.
- **Run the same motion 50 times.** 50 sales conversations with the 10 logos and their networks. The grind is the point — repetition, not novelty.
- **Build in public.** Weekly post on the one domain. Distribution = audience, not just sales.
- **Anti-pigeonhole guardrail.** The 12-month commitment is finite and deliberate — you chose the domain, the timeline, and the exit. BVXpress was indefinite and imposed. Write the exit criteria now: what the domain has to produce by month 12 for you to deepen vs. graduate. This makes the commitment feel bounded, not like a trap.
- **Stakeholder rep (distribution, inward).** Send one written weekly update to whoever you answer to (cofounder, CEO, investor, advisor, or even your accountability partner): what moved the number, what you're asking for, what you're deciding. Pre-align *before* the decision, not after.
- **Leading metric:** conversations/week; first paying logos; weekly update sent without fail. **Lagging:** ARR moving off zero.
- **Explicitly NOT doing:** no architecture rewrites, no "the real problem is actually..." pivots.

### Q3 (Months 7–9) — Leverage through people, not systems
*Improvement area: distribution + people.*

- **Get leverage through a human.** Hire or formally manage at least one person against the number — a contractor SDR, a junior eng, a part-time ops hire. The skill you're building is *output through someone else*, not output through your own hands.
- **Own the number, don't admire it.** Weekly: what moved ARR, what didn't. Cut anything clever that didn't move it.
- **Compound, don't reset.** Same domain, same product, deeper. Resist the Q3 boredom — that boredom is the exact signal you usually act on. This time, don't.
- **Run a real backlog (anti-feature-factory rep).** Keep one visible backlog where *every item names the number it moves and by how much* — no number, no build. Groom it weekly, enforce a WIP limit (max 1–2 things in flight), and keep a **"no" log** of everything you declined and why. This trains the disciplined PM craft you've avoided: prioritization under constraint, not building from whichever reframe excited you that week.
- **Stakeholder cadence at full strength.** Now that a person and customers depend on the number, run a standing weekly alignment: written update, explicit asks, and at least one "disagree-and-commit" decision you sit with instead of re-litigating. Managing the human and the customers *is* the rep — not a distraction from the work.
- **Leading metric:** one person managed against the goal; backlog items 100% tied to the number; weekly stakeholder update streak. **Lagging:** ARR at ~50%+ of target.
- **Explicitly NOT doing:** no new vertical, no "this would work great for healthcare too," no backlog item that can't name its number.

### Q4 (Months 10–12) — Outcome over insight, finish the curve
*Improvement area: outcome-maximization.*

- **Push one number to a real result.** This is the unglamorous 80% you always exit before. Stay in it. Close the gap to target through repetition, not reinvention.
- **Write the case study only after the number is real.** One deep proof point with a *large, verified* number beats ten clever small ones. This becomes the spine of the next-year identity.
- **Decide the next 12 months from a position of compounding, not restlessness.** Renew the same bet or graduate it — but only after holding it a full year.
- **Leading metric:** retention/expansion of existing logos. **Lagging:** ARR at/near target.
- **Explicitly NOT doing:** no new identity, no portfolio rebuild, no fresh domain — even if it feels exciting. *Especially* if it feels exciting.

## The standing rules (guardrails against your own operating system)

- **No new domain for 12 months.** Curiosity gets a notes file, not your calendar.
- **No new identity for 12 months.** One title, one story, one site.
- **Insight only counts if it moves the number.** A beautiful reframe that doesn't move ARR is a hobby this year.
- **Build is rationed; distribution is unlimited.** When in doubt, sell, hire, or post — don't build. Distribution is a muscle you already have; the work is picking it back up, not learning it from scratch.
- **Boredom is the trap, not the signal.** The quarter you most want to leave is the quarter that would have compounded. Stay.
- **No number, no backlog item.** Every prioritized item names the metric it moves and the expected size. Anything that can't is a hobby — it goes to the parking lot, not the sprint. This is your standing defense against the feature factory.
- **Stakeholder management is internal distribution — do it on a schedule.** One written update a week to whoever you answer to, asks stated explicitly, alignment sought *before* decisions. Treat it as non-optional reps, the same as sales calls.
- **Keep a "no" log.** Write down what you declined and why. If the log is empty, you're running a feature factory, not a backlog.
- **Weekly cadence:** every Monday, look at the one number and answer three questions — *did it move?*, *did I do distribution (external **and** internal) or hide in building?*, and *did every build this week trace to the number?*

## The Tactical Outreach (GTM) Playbook

To execute the outbound motion for the **Founding Product Lead** identity:

### Phase 1: Build the List
Filter for companies matching all five criteria:
1. **Stage:** Series-A (occasionally well-funded Seed or early Series-B).
2. **Product:** AI-native in their core product solving workflow complexity.
3. **Execution:** 5+ engineers already in place.
4. **Recency:** Founded 2020-2024.
5. **Location:** North America with remote tolerance.

*Sourcing channels:*
- **Crunchbase:** Filter by sector (logistics, supply chain, ops) + funding stage + raise date (last 18 months).
- **YC Directory:** Search batch 2021-2024 for "logistics", "ops", or "AI".
- **VC Portfolios:** Look at portfolio pages of a16z, Bessemer, Initialized, Lerer Hippeau, and logistics-focused funds (8VC, Trucks VC).
- **LinkedIn:** Search for headcount 10-50 in relevant sectors.

Target size: **50–80 companies in North America.**

### Phase 2: Outreach Execution
Do not wait for job postings. Most early-stage companies hire PMs through network/direct outreach.
- **Audience:** Message the founder at Series-A; Head of Product or VP Engineering at Series-B.
- **Cadence:** Run batches of 15 at a time. Personalize each message.
- **LinkedIn Cold Script Template:**
  > "I built a CV + OR quoting system for last-mile logistics at Quotely, reducing job costing from 60 minutes to 3. I see you're working on [specific problem]. Worth a conversation?"
  *(No resume attachment in the first message. Focus on domain relevance.)*

## What success and failure look like

- **Success:** twelve months from now you have *one* number large enough that you don't need to recycle the old metrics, *one* identity a stranger could repeat back to you, and *one* domain you went deep enough in to have a non-obvious edge. Less interesting on paper. Far more successful.
- **Failure (the relapse):** by month 5 you've "discovered" a more interesting problem in a new domain, started a new prototype, and quietly let the number drift. If you catch yourself doing this, re-read `fatalism.md`. That is the operating system reasserting itself — not a real opportunity.

The greed this file asks for is narrow, not wide: want *one* thing badly enough to hold it through the part that bores you. That single act — not more insight, not more range — is the whole difference between mildly successful and successful.
