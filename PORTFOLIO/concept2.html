How I Evaluate Models

Model choice is a product decision. I treat eval design the same way I treat feature design: fixed inputs, explicit graders, and tradeoffs stated before anyone ships.

The CV quoting testbench

Before scaling automated quoting at Moovez, I built a testbench that scored Gemini Flash and GPT-4.1 on three dimensions that mattered in production:

  • Accuracy: did volume and weight-class estimates match dispatcher ground truth on held-out jobs?
  • Latency: could the model return a usable estimate within the checkout window customers expected (under 2 minutes)?
  • Cost: what did each inference cost at expected booking volume?

The testbench ran on fixed job exhibits, not ad-hoc prompts. Same photos, same expected outputs, same scoring rubric every run. That let me compare models on product-relevant tradeoffs instead of benchmark leaderboard scores.

Eval design principles I apply

  • Fixed exhibits, not free-form chat. Production users hit the same job types repeatedly. Evals should too.
  • Graders before models. Define what "correct" means for each dimension before picking a winner.
  • Three-way tradeoff framing. Accuracy, latency, and cost rarely align. Make the tradeoff explicit so the roadmap conversation is about product constraints, not model religion.
  • Instrumentation at build time. Segment, Heap, and Mixpanel events wired into the prototype so post-launch iteration has baseline data, not guesswork.

Why a PM Who Builds

Prototypes kill bad roadmap bets faster than decks. When I shadowed dispatcher reconciliation at BVXpress, telemetry showed fewer than 20% weekly active users despite strong demo feedback. Building the instrumentation surfaced the real friction point before another quarter of wrong features shipped.

I default to building because it closes the loop between product judgment and production reality. A working CV pipeline answers "will customers trust this?" faster than a PRD. A testbench answers "which model?" faster than a vendor demo. Instrumentation designed at build time means post-launch iteration is evidence-driven from day one.

The question is never "can we use AI here?" It is "what does correct look like, and how will we know when we have it?"
